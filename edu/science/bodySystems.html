body Systems

What are multicellular Organisms?

Organisms that have more than one cell.

Levels of structural organisation

Organelle → Cell → Tissue → Organ → System → Organism

Organelle: Parts of cells (e.g. mitochondria)
Cell: Multiple organelles make up a cell, a cell has a specific job. (e.g. muscle cells)
Tissue: A group of similar cells that have the same goal.
Organ: A group of tissues working together for a specialised job. (e.g. heart)
System: A group of organs that work to perform similar functions. (e.g nervous system)
*Systems are interconnected meaning if one of them fails then all do.*
Organism: A group of systems that work to make a full organism.

Organic & Inorganic

Organic are foods that contain carbon and hydrogen, these are made by living organisms on the other hand everything else classified as inorganic foods (dont contain carbon and hydrogen).

Digestive System

What is the digestive system?

A group of organs that are responsible for breaking down food to be consumed as energy. It also allows energy to be absorbed and used as energy for cells.

Alimentary canal - A tube that runs from the mouth to the anus. This is what food passes through.
Physical digestion - Breaking food up normally
Chemical Digestion - Breaking food up with enzymes or any other chemical

Digestion

[Ingestion]
Mouth - Breaks into chucks, saliva glands produce saliva to break down the food.
Oesophagus - Pipe from mouth to stomach
Stomach - Breaks down into a semi-liquid paste
Small Intestine - Allows energy to be harvested and to be sent throughout the blood
Large Intestine - Allows the last bits of energy to pass through in the same way
Rectum - Waste is thrown out
[Egestion] (faeces)

Excretory System

What is Excretory System?

The elimination of cellular waste from the body. This system does not involve removing undigested food as faeces.

Main Types of Cellular Waste

- Carbon Dioxide
- Urea
- Water

Organs

- Lungs: Excrete carbon dioxide, regulating acid-base balance.
- Liver: Convert ammonia to urea and detoxify substances.
- Kidneys: Filter blood to remove waste and regulate fluid and electrolyte balance.
- Skin: Excrete sweat, removing water, salts, and some urea, while helping regulate body temperature.

Circulatory System

What is the Circulatory System?

The circulatory system is the process of blood and how it moves around the body.

What is blood?

Technically blood is a tissue by the definition meaning but it is a flow of liquid that caries energy and “things” all around the body.

The Blood Vessels

Arteries

Transports blood away from the heart. (high pressure)

Veins

Transports blood towards the heart. (low pressure)

Capillaries

Capillaries carry blood in between arteries & veins; location of gas exchange.

Types of Blood

Plasma

- 55% of blood is plasma.
- The purpose is to move all the substances besides oxygen. This includes waters, salts, nutrients, hormones and proteins to different parts of the body that need it.
- `Straw-coloured liquid mainly made out of water.

Red Blood Cells (RBCs, Erythrocytes)

- They have no nucleus.
- They are essentially packets of oxygen that help cells remain alive.
- Their shape can be described as biconcave disc-shaped cells.

White Blood Cells (WBCs)

- They have a large nucleus, much bigger than RBCs, lower quantity to RBCs.
- Their purpose is to help in the immune system.

Types of WBCs

- Phagocytes: Kill pathogens by engulfing them.
- Lymphocytes: Produces antibodies

Platelets

- Cell fragments that help with blood clotting.

Circulation Pathways

- (Systemic System, High pressure, arteries) De-oxygenated blood pumps from the left heart up to the lunges capillaries. - (Pulmonary System) The capillaries remove the carbon dioxide from the blood and puts oxygen there instead. Now being veins (low pressure), the blood moves back to the right heart. - (Systemic System) The blood full of oxygen is pumped into the rest of the body and comes back via arteries by other capillaries spread around the body.

Types of Gas Exchange

- External - Exchange of gases between alveoli and capillaries. - Internal - Exchange of gases between body cells and capillaries.

Respiratory System

Nasal Cavity

- Where air is inhaled and exhaled
- The nose has hair which blocks out a lot the incoming pathogens.

Pharynx

- Back of the throat

Trachea

- A tube of smooth muscle that expands and contracts.
- Leads to the lungs from the throat.
- Cilla surrounds the trachea which is a traps for incoming and unwanted particles.

Bronchi

- An air passage that diverts the air from the trachea into the lungs.
- Cilla surrounds the trachea which is a traps for incoming and unwanted particles.

Bronchioles

- Extensions of Bronchi that allow air to reach deeper in the lungs.

Alveoli

- Air sacks that consume oxygen as energy and produce carbon dioxide as a waste product.
- This is also where external gas exchange happens (covered in the circulatory system), and therefore energy is transferred to the blood.

Blood

- The carrier of energy to other cells in the body which require oxygen as a food source.

The Nervous System

What is the nervous system?

Network of cells that allows animals to respond rapidly to their environment.
- Detects changes
- Decides what to do
- Does it (muscle)

Neuron

- The fundamental unit of the nervous system.
- They transmit information via impulses (electrical)

Nerve

A bundle of neurons in a wire like form.

Central Nervous System (CNS)

- Includes neurons in spinal cord and brain.

Peripheral Nervous System (PNS)

- Neurons outside the brain and spinal cord.
- Carries signals away and towards the CNS.

Sympathetic Nervous System

A sub-section of the nervous system that control flight or flight repsonses. It is in the middle of the spinal cord.

Stimulus-Response Pathway

- [PNS] Sensory Neuron: Detects stimuli in our skin and sends it to the spinal cord.
- [CNS] Interneuron: After the spinal cord receives the signal it sends it the brain which asses it and (the brain) sends a response. Then the interneuron sends a signal to a motor neuron.
- [PNS] Motor Neuron: The motor neuron uses the muscle.

Types of neurons

Sensory Neurons

Detect stimuli and send to interneurons (PNS, Receptor).

Interneuron/Relay Neuron

Connects sensory and motor neurons with the brain (CNS).

Motor Neuron

Neurons that perform a physical action e.g. moving a muscle (PNS, Effector).

Reflex Arc

When something sudden happens to your body, your body requires a rapid response. In this case when the sensory neuron detects it, it sends it to the interneuron which instantly sends it to a motor neuron to defend yourself. Afterwards the motor neuron sends back a information report to the interneuron which sends it to the brain. In this case the interneuron didn't send the signal to the brain to be processed but rather instantly sent it to a motor neuron and sends it after. Reflex arcs are a involuntary responses while critically thinking about something is a voluntary response.

Endocrine System

What is the endocrine system?

The endocrine system consists of a group of glands that secrete hormones.

Hormone (Chemical Messengers)

A small chemical which causes a response in another region of the body by altering the activity of cells, this travel through the blood

Glands (Producers)

Organs that are specialised for the production and release of hormones.

Types of Glands (ChatGPT)

Pituitary Gland

Function: Master gland regulating other glands; produces growth hormone, thyroid-stimulating hormone, etc.
Location: Base of the brain.

Thyroid Gland

Function: Regulates metabolism, energy production, and growth through thyroid hormones.
Location: Front of the neck, below the Adam's apple.

Adrenal Glands

Function: Produces hormones like adrenaline, cortisol, and aldosterone; helps manage stress.
Location: On top of each kidney.

Pancreas

Function: Produces insulin and glucagon, regulating blood sugar levels.
Location: Behind the stomach.

Ovaries (females)

Function: Produces oestrogen` and progesterone; regulates menstrual cycle and reproduction.
Location: In the pelvic cavity, one on each side of the uterus.

Testes (males)

Function: Produces testosterone; regulates sperm production and male characteristics.
Location: In the scrotum.

Disease

What is Disease?

A condition that impairs the normal functioning of an organism.

Types of Disease

Communicable (Infectious)

- Spreadable from one organism to another.
- Communicable diseases are always caused by pathogens.

Non-Communicable (Non-Infectious)

- Not spreadable from one organism to another.
- Non-Communicable diseases are never caused by pathogens.

Direct & Indirect

Direct

When an infected individual transmits pathogens to another person through physical touch or bodily fluids.

Indirect

When an infected person contaminates items that another person subsequently touches, such as doorknobs or utensils.

Types of Pathogens

Lines of Defence

Innate: A generalised or non-specific response to all cells. (given at birth).
Adaptive: A specific response to kill a certain type of pathogen. (developed overtime).

Line 1 (Innate)

Skin: Dry and strong blocks 99% of pathogens.
Mucous Membrane: A sticky slime that will catch anything in that touches it, mucous surrounds any opening in the body.
Cilia: Hair like, something that sweeps mucous to be coughed out or swallowed.

Line 2 (Innate)

Phagocytes: They engulf and destroy foreign or unwanted material using enzymes.
Inflammation: When blood surrounds an infected area (white blood cells).
Fever: Making your body hotter so that the pathogen dies from heat.

Line 3 (Adaptive)

Antibodies: They cling onto pathogens and malfunction them, (each antigen has a different antibody).
Last Line:It is not instantly activated and only is active when the first and second line fail for the immune system.
Memory CellsIt also remembers the pathogens it encounters using memeory cells.
